### The Beginning

It all started with the economic situation in the United States after the dot-com crisis and the 9/11 attacks in New York. These events drastically worsened market conditions.

The NASDAQ lost 72% of its value by 2002, and the S&P 500 dropped 14% on the day of the Twin Towers attack. Many companies went bankrupt and had to lay off workers, pushing unemployment to 6%. This, in turn, led people to cut expenses, withdraw money from banks, and thus the recession chain was set in motion.

![Chart](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F21b023ae-a24f-4dfd-8443-e1a3f5383b07_859x407.png)

The only way to restore consumption and re-stimulate the economy was through expansionary policies by the Federal Reserve. This way, credit became cheaper, and companies had more liquidity to restore, expand, and hire workers‚Äîhelping to revive consumer spending.

These policies brought interest rates down to as low as 1% by 2003, setting the stage for a new economic cycle and the perfect conditions for another bubble.

### The Housing Market

Given the situation we just described, credit became extremely accessible. If you borrowed $100, you had to return $100 + $1. That promoted a scenario where everyone began taking out loans for things like student debt, personal loans, business funding, and especially home purchases‚Äî**mortgages**.

James and his wife saw two things:

### Everything's Fine‚Ä¶ Until It Isn‚Äôt

As shown in the example above, banks held thousands of mortgages they couldn‚Äôt afford to keep until maturity‚Äîthey needed immediate liquidity to continue the lending cycle.

So they sold these mortgages to financial institutions, which created special products that could be bought by investors.

In short:  
The mortgage went from the bank to a financial institution, and this institution sold a special package to investors so they could participate in the real estate market and benefit from the mortgage payments‚Äîwithout owning the home or the loan directly.

These investors bought special packages called **MBS (Mortgage-Backed Securities)** and made money as long as the borrowers made their monthly payments.
![Chart 3 ](https://images.surferseo.art/f9a7e638-123f-46b1-a9b7-af75ce51936b.png)
But here‚Äôs the problem:  
If borrowers stopped paying, the MBS would default. Investors would sell off the MBS quickly, since they lost value.

The credit chain weakened because the underlying asset‚Äîthe houses‚Äîstarted to lose value.

Home prices had gone up without solid fundamentals, often for homes in distant, undesirable areas.

At some point, buyers stopped purchasing homes because they were too expensive. Meanwhile, wages didn‚Äôt increase, and jobs were still mostly unskilled.

As a result, the guarantees behind the loans became insufficient, and risks weren‚Äôt properly covered.

### The Clever Michael Burry

As we mentioned, these MBS products were made of **subprime mortgages**. That means banks had lent money to people who couldn‚Äôt afford to repay their debts. When those people defaulted, banks were left holding toxic assets‚Äîbecause the collateral (the houses) had also lost value.
![Chart 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Mortgage_delinquencies_by_loan_type_-_1998-2010.GIF/500px-Mortgage_delinquencies_by_loan_type_-_1998-2010.GIF)
This situation led to multiple bank bankruptcies and widespread defaults.

But Michael Burry and his team analyzed the assets behind those MBS before the crash. They realized they were mostly subprime mortgages.

So, they approached several banks to negotiate **insurance** against defaults on those mortgages‚Äîessentially placing a bet that people would not be able to pay them.

They negotiated what‚Äôs called a **CDS (Credit Default Swap)**‚Äîa contract where two parties agree to exchange capital flows if a specific event occurs.

---
